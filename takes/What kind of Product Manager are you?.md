Product management is one of those capabilities that can make or break the greatness of a product. They provide the umbrella under which different capabilities like engineering, design, operations, marketing, sales, leadership etc., can come together. But product management is such a fluid role, that it’s difficult to know who can be a good PM and who wouldn’t. Over a period of time, I have developed my own mental model and this is my attempt to put my thoughts on paper.
Before we jump in, let’s set some measurement. I will assign a number of 1-4 (1 being lesser and 4 being the highest) in terms of capability in a particular competency. 
Secondly, I am not going assume that any product manager will be known by both their strategy and execution capabilities. A product manager who only does strategy will have no impact to showcase, while a pure execution PM is no different from a consultant.
# Different levels of Product Managers
Even though there are many way to identify the different levels of a PM, for me there are three levels of a PM.
## The regular PM
These are your everyday PMs that have been forcible moved into the role by leadership to give them a better prospect. Don’t get me wrong, there is nothing wrong with this. But I would argue that the regular PMs bring a very specific capability to the job role and setting wrong expectation just because a new name was assigned doesn’t set up the person to be successful. So it’s very important that there is right expectation mapped for each of these roles and enable them to grow in the future.
## The top 10% PM
These are your capable people, who come with certain core capability along with the intention to be great in the product management role in all it’s different dimension. I will elaborate more on what these dimensions are a little later, but these are folks who are capable of being true product leaders and build memorable experiences for the customers.
## The top 1% PM
These are the special people who are capable of building the next rocket ship, wearing multiple hats at the same time and will make sure they deliver greatness as a PM. There are very few people who are like this from day 1, but not having them will make product journeys difficult than it should be.
# Different competencies of a Product Manager
![[PM_Competency.png]]
Even though there could 1 less or 10 more competencies that I could have listed, but to make it easier and be able to cover 99% of PM evaluations, I would look at 4 competency categories that each PM needs to be evaluated. 
## Domain competency
The core hypothesis about domain competency is that the people with deep understanding of domain will be able to provide the product leadership. For example, people with deep understanding of payment process will be able to provide product leadership for Fintech products. Domain competency takes a lot of time to develop and takes time by exposure to business problems and deep research.
## Product Management
The core hypothesis about product management competency is that the process of developing a product is a competency category in itself. For example, a PM needs to know how to define a problem, perform user research, competitor analysis, roadmapping, pricing, revenue, marketing etc. Each one is a discipline of its own and needs learning and exposure to gain experience and understanding.
## Software engineering
The core hypothesis about software engineering is that the people with deep understanding of software engineering will be able to provide product leadership. Every software engineer needs to have a deep understanding of the problem statement before they can build the software which provides value to customers. I am also trying to draw a difference between software engineer and developer. But I am not the right person to clarify this difference.
## Humanity
The core hypothesis here is that the folks who have deep understanding of humanity will be able to provide product leadership. I am also using this as a catch all category with disciplines like Pyschology, Design, Art etc to be part of the category. It also covers the soft skills required by the PMs to successfully deliver impact.
# Different types of PM by capabilities
### Generalist Product Manager
Also known as the “Get S*** Done” PMs, they are the best PMs to have at the start of any product journey. They have the attitude of execute first and ask questions later. Since the problem statements are usually blurry at the start of any product journey, they bring great clarity and velocity to get the v1 of the product.
### Pros
1. A rare breed and these folks are hard to come by. They are usually behind great products or also shaped by great products. They will showcase great impact from day 1.
2. They are usually have high competencies on product management, humanities and software engineering.
### Cons
1. They are rare breed to come by and are hard to come by.
2. They are usually the Grade A personalities who will take “No” for an answer. This might sound like a good thing but they become a liability in large organizations which have different power centers.
## Design Product Manager
A design PM or UX PM is one who has great design capabilities and is able to visualize the product based on the problem definition. They usually have design background or some kind of design portfolio and have been part of companies where they took up the role of PM.
### Pros
1. Great personas who can get the initial roadblocks sorted out and get leadership and design partners onboard to direction.
2. Will fastrack time to market by huge margin since they will be able to prototype in design tools like Figma.
### Cons
1. Since design is a discipline of its own, the chances of having great domain and software engineering skills will be low and hence middle part of execution will be messy.
2. This persona definitely needs a strong engineering partner who can work to get the product which is customer worthy.
## Growth Product Manager
Growth PMs are more focused on customer acquisition than the actual product execution. This role is high on Humanities and Domain competencies, OK on Product Management competency  but very low on domain and software engineering competencies.
### Pros
1. Must have for any company which has found the PMF and need to take off. They will be able to push the scaling journey to a higher gear.
2. They know the customers and ICP very intimately and will be able to craft the product strategy to align well towards acquiring these customers.
### Cons
1. These PMs will be hard sell for 0-1 journey since they will not be able to showcase high impact.
2. Growth PMs need to be supported well by leadership for them to be effectively since they will rub against Sales and Marketing folks and in any good company, these are the folks who come with strong characters.
## Technical Product Manager
Also called Platform Product Manager, these are the folks who are great fit for companies who have found product market fit and bring solid product management and software engineering competencies. They are usually the folks who rather build a platform rather than a product.
### Pros
1. These folks are very good for working closely with engineering teams and able to shape the problems statement in a way that makes sense for engineers.
2. They think long term and focus more on foundations rather than features. This helps in building capabilities that might become super power for the product.
### Cons
1. These PMs need a space and time to built a complex system without which they will not be able to shine.
2. If the organisation is not aligned on the platform approach, very hard for these folks to showcase impact.
## Data Product Manager / AI Product Manager
The new age product managers who are critical for large product organizations to deliver value and impact once the low hanging fruits are gone. These are strategic roles which will help with the experimentation journeys and hopefully deliver customer value somewhere along the journey.
### Pros
1. Absolutely required in the latest age of Data and AI to maintain the strategic edge that all companies are building.
2. They are able to bring data into decision making and might be able to deliver key capability as a data product or a AI feature.
### Cons
1. It’s very hard to define the exact impact or outcome for these roles, since they operate with open ended problem statements.
2. They will not be able to operate alone and hence need a well structured product management organization to support them.
## Product Marketing Manager
I blame Brian Chesky for making this role hot. They are the folks who craft the strategy to sell the product and work very closely with Marketing team. They have the marketing roadmap for the product and influence the product as “Voice of Customer”.
### Pros
1. They become “Voice of Customer” and provide clarity for the build team.
2. They are able to communicate clearly with leadership, sales and marketing teams to put together the roadmap for selling and generating revenue.
### Cons
1. This is a hard role to define as a product manager since they certainly do not have product management and software engineering competencies. They are high on humanities and domain competencies.
2. Either they have a big dependency on engineering team or the lead who is leading the build team. They will not be able to drive the execution at ground level.
# Conclusion
To get the right PM, it’s very important to have the clarity on what is required and how much support exists at that point of time. If you hire a design PM and don’t have enough engineering chops, you are going to be left high and dry after the initial excitement. If you hire a data PM and have no supporting Product Manager, the data PM will struggle to get any features delivered to the customer.
The way I would approach the PM hiring is to evaluate what type of PM I need and then try to find whether they are Top 1% PM, Top 10% PM or regular PM. The combination of both takes about 3 months to 1 year time but it’s been a good framework for me to approach this problem.
